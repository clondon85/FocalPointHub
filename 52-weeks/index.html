52 Weeks with C. London · 2026 Edition

Welcome to year 8.

What started as a small project of self-motivation has grown into an incredibly supportive community of photographers at every level, spread all over the world. This year brings back the monthly Artist Spotlight, a bonus prompt featuring a photographer to draw inspiration from.

The purpose of the challenge is to make new photos with the prompt in mind, not to dig through your archive for something that fits.

May: Proximity

There's an old adage in photography: if your photos aren't good enough, you're not close enough. It's attributed to Robert Capa, debated endlessly, and true often enough to stick around. This month uses distance as the primary variable across four prompts. How close you are to your subject shapes what you can see, what gets lost, and what the viewer feels standing in front of the image.

At arm's reach

Many photographers default to staying back at a comfortable distance. Try the opposite. Get close enough that you could touch what you are photographing, and maybe give macro a try.

Kept at a distance

Find something you cannot get to, or that is being kept from getting to you. A fence, a window, a velvet rope, a crowd with a clear center.

Look at the separation as the subject, not an obstacle to work around.

Crowded

Find a scene where things are packed in tight. People in a group, objects on a shelf, buildings with no space between them. Pay attention to what this compactness does to the details.

What gets obscured, what gets forced into contact, what you stop being able to read clearly.

Isolated

Find one subject with real, deliberate space around it. A single figure on an empty street, an animal with no herd, an object cleared away from everything else.

Consider what that kind of alone communicates before you press the shutter.

Artist spotlight
Levon Biss
A British photographer best known for his large-scale macro work documenting insects and botanical specimens in extraordinary detail. Each image is assembled from thousands of individual photographs, revealing structures invisible to the naked eye and presented at print sizes up to three meters tall.

April: Witness

Photography is an act of bearing witness. This month, four prompts push you to photograph with presence and intention: other people, overlooked places, traces of the past, and a full day of your own life.

Unseen

Photograph someone or something that exists in plain sight but goes unnoticed. The overlooked person on a busy street, the building everyone passes without looking up, the object that has been in the same spot so long it has become invisible.

Your job is to make the viewer see it for the first time.

Eye contact

Make a portrait where the subject meets the lens directly. A person, an animal, even a statue.

Something shifts when a subject acknowledges the camera. Sit with that tension and decide what you want it to mean.

Evidence

Photograph the trace of something, not the thing itself. A worn path, an empty chair, a window with condensation, a meal half eaten.

Show what was there without showing it directly.

A day in the life

Choose one day this month and document it fully, from the moment you wake up until right before you fall asleep. Aim for at least one photo an hour.

You are not trying to make it look good. You are trying to make it true.

Artist spotlight
Zanele Muholi
A South African visual activist and photographer whose work centres on Black queer identity, visibility, and dignity. Their series Faces and Phases and Somnyama Ngonyama are among the most powerful bodies of documentary portraiture of the past two decades. Work that is explicitly about the act of witnessing and being witnessed. The connection to this month's theme is direct and intentional.
Official site Wikipedia Tate profile
March: In Between

March sits mid-stride, not quite one thing, not yet another. Work with the threshold, the almost, the incomplete. Lean into the ambiguity.

Caught mid-change

Find something that has not finished becoming what it is going to be. A bud that has not opened. A building half-demolished. A face mid-expression.

The incompleteness is the whole point. Fight every instinct to wait for it to resolve and shoot it as-is.

Threshold

Doorways, shorelines, the edge of a shadow, the moment before someone walks through a door.

A threshold implies both sides at once, and your job is to put that tension in the frame.

Neither here nor there

Find a scene that refuses to be one thing and do not try to fix it in post. Dawn or dusk, joy or grief, abandoned or just quiet.

Ambiguity is welcome here.

Before it's gone

Steam off a coffee. Chalk on a sidewalk before it rains. That look someone gives right before they realise you are photographing them.

Find something small and impermanent and make it last.

Artist spotlight
Saul Leiter
Leiter spent decades photographing New York through rain-streaked windows, around reflections, in the margins of scenes where the real action had already happened or had not quite started yet. His images are soft, layered, and deliberately hard to read. If you have never come across his work, clear your afternoon.
Wikipedia Official Foundation
February: Limited Light

As the days get shorter, light becomes limited and more intentional. Work with those conditions using motion, stillness, and unconventional light sources to shape meaning. Pay attention to what changes when the light fades. That is where the interesting stuff lives.

Show motion

Light trails, blurred commuters, soft water. Use a slow shutter speed to show motion. You will need a steady hand, tripod, or solid surface.

Can not control your shutter speed? Think creatively about other ways to suggest movement.

Unconventional light

Some of the most interesting light comes from unconventional places, a bonfire, a TV set, car headlights.

The main light source should come from something other than the sun or standard light bulbs.

One light

One artificial light source only. A table lamp or phone flashlight will do just fine.

Subject is up to you, no cheating with multiple sources.

Still, Life

Interpret as you will. Still life the genre. Life being still. Life still going on.

However you read this, you do you. The ambiguity is intentional.

Artist spotlight
Gordon Parks
Parks used low light as a narrative tool rather than a limitation. Working largely in available light, often indoors or at night, he embraced shadow, contrast, and darkness to convey emotional weight and social reality. His photographs show how restraint in light can heighten intimacy, tension, and meaning.
Wikipedia Foundation archive
January: Getting Started

Four prompts chosen to help you feel motivated, engaged, and intentional about the year ahead. All focused on helping you identify how to make the most of the project.

Setting goals

Think about what you want to achieve this year. Set a goal and make a photograph which demonstrates it.

You will revisit this midway through and at the end of the year.

Your passion

Make a photo that shows what you love. Be as concrete or abstract as you like.

Highlight what makes this your passion so a viewer can interpret your relationship to it.

Something familiar

Find something you interact with every day and photograph it from at least ten different angles. Choose the best one.

What is mundane to you may be fascinating to another.

Past experiences

Show where you are in your photographic journey. Wildlife, portraits, street, sport, photograph what you like, how you normally would.

You have free range here.

Artist spotlight
Martin Parr
Known for vibrant colours and hyper-real documentation of the everyday. Often comical, his well-placed colours elevate the mundane to something with true character. We lost Martin last month, and as he has been an enormous inspiration, it felt only right to start the year with him.
